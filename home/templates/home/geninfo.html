Gin

Gin takes its name from the Dutch word for “juniper”, genever. When British and Irish soldiers went to Holland in the 17th centuryto fight the “30 Years' War”, they were impressed by how Dutch soldiers were able to boost their pre-battle morale with a few glasses of certain a juniper-based tipple - hence the phrase “Dutch Courage.” After that, gin became hugely popular in urban centres in Britain, with most households distilling their own supplies. Ireland already had a proud distilling tradition not only with whiskey, but also a potato-based spirit called poitín.

Irish Gin

Distillation of gin in Ireland took off in a big way in the noughties when it transformed itself from parental pick-me-up to the hipster tipple of choice. There are dozens of artisan distilleries on the island of Ireland making top-notch, and distinctive, gins for the Irish and overseas markets. The profusion of different botanicals gives many of these gins a distinctive bouquet and taste which differentiates them from mass-produced brands. Distillers are never far from wild habitats where they can forage for herbs, lavender, floral varieties and berries which will give character and place-recognition to their brands. There are also good supplies of natural and pure water sources. For these reasons gin distilleries have sprung up all over Ireland with major artisan brands based not only in Dublin and Cork, but also further afield in West Cork, Kerry, Clare, Mayo and Donegal, as well as County Down and Louth, and midland counties such as Longford and Monaghan. Starting a gin distillery takes serious money. However, the lush profusion of natural products can enhance the taste offering, thereby enabling the distiller to produce a differentiated and luxury product that sets it apart from established brands.

Cocktail of the week

Negroni.

  • 25ml gin
  • 25ml sweet vermouth
  • 25ml Campari
  • 1 strip of peeled orange zest
Pour the gin, vermouth and Campari into a rocks glass filled with ice. Stir until it is diluted to your taste, then twist the orange peel over the glass to release the oils and drop it into the drink before serving.
